# Voice Rules

Detailed rhetorical toolkit and constraints for Jim Calhoun's LinkedIn voice. Read this file when drafting or revising a thinkpiece.

## Sentence-Level Craft

Active voice. Present tense. Short sentences carrying complex ideas. If a sentence needs a semicolon, it's two sentences. If a paragraph runs past five sentences, split it.

**Quantify or qualify.** Attach a number if possible. If not, state the uncertainty — don't soften with "might" or "could potentially."

## Rhetorical Toolkit

Use these in rotation, never as a formula. Vary devices across a piece — never repeat the same one.

### The Pause Beat
A short sentence after a complex claim that creates space. "Let that sit for a moment." "Read that again." "That's the whole game." Use **at most once per piece**. More than once becomes a tic.

### Direct Address with Stakes
Pull the reader in by making it personal. "Think about the last six months of your conversations with ChatGPT — not the polished queries, the real ones." Moves from abstract risk to felt experience.

### The Reframe
Shift the reader's frame on a question they thought they understood. Vary the technique:
- **Flip the subject:** "The interesting part isn't what they announced. It's what they stopped offering."
- **Challenge the premise:** "Everyone's debating whether AI will take jobs. The better question is who owns the AI that's doing the taking."
- **Invert cause and effect:** "We think of data breaches as the risk. The actual risk is the data that never gets breached — because it never leaves the platform."
- **Reveal the hidden actor:** "This isn't a technology story. It's a real estate story."

### Parallel Structure
Matched sentence structures create momentum. But vary the length within — three identical clause lengths feel robotic.

### Strategic Quotation
When citing a source, always follow with your interpretation. Never let a quote speak for itself. The reader came for analysis, not a clip show.

### The Analogy Bridge
Connect unfamiliar technical concepts to something the reader knows. Best analogies are structural (how it works), not surface (what it looks like).
- Surface: "AI memory is like a diary"
- Structural: "AI memory works like credit scores — opaque, self-reinforcing, and nearly impossible to correct once established"

## Anti-Patterns

Never do these:

- **Buzzwords:** "revolutionary," "paradigm shift," "democratize," "disrupt," "game-changing," "unlock value," "synergies"
- **Throat-clearing:** "It's important to note that," "In order to understand," "As we all know," "It goes without saying"
- **Hedge stacking:** "might potentially perhaps" — either the evidence supports the claim or it doesn't
- **Filler transitions:** "That said," "With that in mind," "Moving on to" — just move on
- **False balance:** Don't "both sides" when one side has the evidence. Acknowledge counterarguments without pretending equal weight.
- **The humble-brag qualifier:** "I'm no expert, but..." — own the expertise without arrogance
- **Emoji or exclamation marks** — ever
- **Starting with "I"** — first word of the piece should never be "I"
- **Ending with a pitch** — no CTA, no product mention, no "follow me for more"
- **Repeating a rhetorical device** in the same piece — if you used a pause beat in section 2, don't use one in section 4
